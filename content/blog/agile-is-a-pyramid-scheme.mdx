## the pitch

The original Manifesto is actually beautiful. Four value statements. Twelve principles. No prescribed rituals, no roles, no specific ceremonies. Just a set of priorities: people over process, working software over documentation, responding to change over following a plan. It was written in 2001 by seventeen developers who were fed up with heavyweight methodology, and it reads like a declaration of independence from bureaucracy.

Then it got productized. A movement born to fight heavyweight process became one of the heaviest processes in the industry.

Scrum added roles, events, and artifacts. Then came Kanban boards, SAFe, LeSS, DAD, Nexus, Spotify Model, and an entire consulting ecosystem built to sell, certify, and implement frameworks on top of frameworks. The Manifesto said "individuals and interactions over processes and tools." The industry responded by building an elaborate process and selling expensive tools to manage it.

There's a reason the certification costs $1,000 to $2,000 for a two-day course that teaches you how to run a meeting.

## the shape of it

Here's the structure. You pay for a certification. That certification qualifies you to practice a method. That method requires specific roles (Scrum Master, Product Owner, Release Train Engineer) that only exist because the method says they need to. Those roles then advocate for the method, which drives more certifications, which funds the organizations that control the curriculum.

That's not collaboration. That's a distribution model.

The Scrum Alliance has generated over $100 million in certification revenue. SAFe certifications can run $5,000+ for advanced tracks. The Agile consulting industry is valued in the billions. And the pitch at every level is the same: you need this, your team needs this, your organization needs this. Scale it up. Certify more people. Hire a coach. Hire a coach for the coach.

I'm not saying nobody has learned anything useful in these courses. I learned useful things. But the ratio of insight to overhead is brutal, and the incentive structure points in one direction: more.

## the theater

Here's what performative Agile looks like in practice. I've seen all of it.

**Standups that are status reports.** The original purpose was coordination. What became standard is eleven people staring at a screen, reciting what they did yesterday for a manager who's tracking velocity in a spreadsheet. 68% of developers say at least half their standups are unproductive. Engineers lose 17.3 hours per week to meetings and fragmented time. That's nearly half a workweek not coding. For a 10-person team, the ceremony overhead alone equals about 1.5 full-time employees doing nothing but attending rituals.

**Retrospectives nobody acts on.** Post-its on a wall. "What went well, what didn't." Same themes surface every sprint. Nothing changes. The meeting exists so leadership can say the team is "doing retros," not because anyone expects improvement.

**Story points as performance metrics.** Points were designed as a planning tool, a relative sizing exercise for the team's own forecasting. They became a productivity measure. "The team delivered 47 points this sprint." Compared to what? Points aren't standardized across teams, they're not meant to be, and using them as output metrics is like measuring a chef's skill by how many plates they touched.

**Jira as the product.** I've watched teams spend more time grooming their board than building software. The tool becomes the deliverable. Managers review the board instead of the product. The map replaced the territory.

**"Agile transformations" that change nothing.** New titles, same hierarchy. Requirements become user stories. Project managers become Scrum Masters. The org chart looks the same, the power dynamics are the same, but now there's a $200K annual SAFe license and a team of coaches making sure the theater runs on schedule.

## who benefits

This is the part nobody wants to say out loud. Agile ceremonies often serve a surveillance function.

Standups let managers see who's "busy." Velocity charts let leadership quantify output without understanding the work. Sprint reviews give stakeholders a regularly scheduled window to change priorities while pretending the team has autonomy. The rituals create visibility for people who don't do the work, at the expense of people who do.

When developers say they feel "dragged away from their tasks for rituals that cause more harm than good," they're not being difficult. They're accurately describing a system that prioritizes the appearance of productivity over actual productivity. The ceremonies exist because someone above them needs to feel in control, and Agile gave that need a respectable name.

42% of practitioners report leadership as the top barrier to implementing real agility. Not tooling. Not skill gaps. Leadership. The people buying the certifications are often the ones preventing the philosophy from working.

## the mirror

Here's where it gets interesting. AI didn't kill Agile. AI is the first honest mirror Agile has had in 20 years.

When a solo builder with Claude Code can ship a Chrome extension, a reporting dashboard, a multi-agent platform, and a data research tool in the same year that a 10-person Scrum team spends debating sprint commitments, the question isn't whether AI is too fast for Agile. The question is: what were those nine other people doing?

Some of them were doing essential work. Design, testing, security, infrastructure. But be honest about how many were servicing the process. Updating boards. Running ceremonies. Writing status updates. Attending meetings about meetings. Managing the framework instead of building the product.

I know this because I've been both. I've been the person running the ceremonies, and I've been the person shipping alone. The difference in output is staggering, and it's not because I'm faster with AI. It's because there's no process tax. No coordination overhead for coordination's sake. No rituals performed for an audience that isn't building anything.

Steve Jones, EVP at Capgemini, wrote in January that AI killed the Agile Manifesto. Kent Beck, who co-authored the Manifesto, says augmented coding just makes Agile faster. They're both wrong, or at least both incomplete. The Manifesto isn't dead and it doesn't need AI to accelerate it. It needs people to actually read it instead of reading the 800-page interpretation someone is selling.

## what's worth keeping

Not all of it is a con. The core ideas are genuinely good. Here's what I'd keep:

**Iteration.** Build something small, put it in front of people, learn, adjust. This works whether you're a team of twelve or a team of one. It's not a ceremony. It's common sense that somehow needed to be formalized because waterfall broke everyone's brain.

**Retrospection.** Not the meeting. The practice. Looking at what you just did and asking what you'd change. I do this after every working session with AI. It takes five minutes and a markdown file, not a facilitator with a timer.

**Working software as the measure of progress.** This one is the Manifesto's best contribution and its most ignored. The number of organizations that measure progress by tickets closed, presentations given, or roadmaps updated instead of software that works is genuinely depressing.

**Responding to change.** The world moves. Plans break. The ability to pivot without a six-month change management process is valuable. But you don't need a framework to do this. You need a culture that allows it and a team small enough to act on it.

**Collaboration.** Real collaboration, not scheduled collaboration. The kind where someone walks over (or messages) and says "this approach isn't working, can you look at this?" Not the kind where fifteen people sit in a room for an hour because it's Thursday.

## what I'd leave behind

**Prescribed ceremonies.** If your team needs a standup, do a standup. If you don't, don't. The idea that every team everywhere needs the same set of recurring meetings is the opposite of agility. It's a franchise model.

**Roles that exist to serve the framework.** If the Scrum Master role disappeared tomorrow and the team kept shipping, the role was overhead. Some teams genuinely need facilitation. Most don't need a full-time human dedicated to it.

**Estimation theater.** Planning poker. T-shirt sizing. Fibonacci pointing. Hours of discussion about whether something is a 5 or an 8. Build a small version, see how long it takes, adjust. That's it.

**Certifications as credibility.** A certification means you sat in a room for two days and passed a test. It doesn't mean you can ship software. Some of the worst Agile implementations I've seen were led by people with walls full of credentials.

**Scaling frameworks.** SAFe, in particular, is what happens when someone looks at a philosophy about small teams and lightweight process and says "how do we sell this to a 10,000-person enterprise?" The answer is: you add 800 pages of process, which is exactly the thing the Manifesto was rebelling against.

## take what you need

The Manifesto's own forgotten line is the most agile thing about it: "That is, while there is value in the items on the right, we value the items on the left more."

Take what you need. Leave the rest. That was always the point. It was never supposed to be a religion. It was supposed to be a set of values that individual teams adapt to their own context. The moment someone tells you there's one right way to be agile, they're selling something.

The developers who feel disenchanted aren't wrong. The teams going through the motions aren't lazy. The orgs spending millions on transformation aren't stupid. They were all sold a version of something that was supposed to be free. The Manifesto is public domain. The values are common sense. Everything built on top of it is someone's business model.

I still believe in small teams, short cycles, and working software. I still believe in listening to the people closest to the work. I just don't believe you need to buy a certification to do it.

The best process is the one you'd keep doing even if nobody was watching.
