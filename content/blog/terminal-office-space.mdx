## boot sequence

Every morning starts the same way. I open three terminal tabs, sometimes four. Each one gets a different repo. I run /onboard, which loads up the project's instructions, architecture, session logs, and whatever was happening last time I touched it. The AI reads all of it before we say a word to each other.

Some days I start with /metrics to see what's happening with traffic or errors. Some days I already know what I'm walking into because I left myself notes. Either way, the CLAUDE.md file gets reviewed, the most recent session log gets read, and then we start working.

It's not dramatic. It's not some futuristic sci-fi thing. It's more like opening up a shop in the morning. Lights on, check the register, see what needs restocking.

## prompt2story almost killed me

My first project was Prompt2Story, and it was a huge pain in the ass.

I'd found Devin.ai first, which could do much more than any of the big box AI companies at the time. I was totally hooked. Got some practice working with Devin until Claude Code came out, and I never looked back. Something about the terminal just felt natural for me. I had previous terminal experience, and it was the type of workflow I'd been looking for without knowing it.

Prompt2Story started in Devin and finished in Claude. And holy shit, what a journey.

Everything was trial and error. I spent hundreds of hours on that project. The learning curve wasn't gentle, it was vertical. I didn't know what I was doing, and the tools were still figuring themselves out too. But I kept going because I wanted to understand how this worked, not just use it.

That frustration was the tuition. Every error, every broken build, every moment where I wanted to throw my laptop taught me something I still use today.

## the solution doesn't exist yet

My favorite part of this work is the problem solving. That's the thing that actually gets me out of bed.

I don't go into a project assuming I have a solution. There's research involved, a lot of it. There are potentials and problem cases and things that might work and things that definitely won't. But the actual solution is something that starts to appear while we're actively working. It's never static. It reveals itself.

We just want to solve it the best, simplest, cleanest, smartest way we can. Sometimes that requires thinking outside of the box and leaving what we thought we knew behind. That's the part that keeps it interesting. You can't get attached to your first idea. Or your second one. The answer is usually the one you didn't walk in with.

I think that's what's so exciting about humans teaming up with AI. We're going to stumble upon new ways of doing things. Fewer limitations. Methods that didn't exist a year ago becoming standard practice. The ceiling keeps moving.

## it's wrong again

AI gets things wrong. A lot.

It's frustrating, but I've learned to take a step back when it happens because the tool is more helpful than not. It's ok that it isn't perfect. I have guardrails in place to keep us on track and to make sure we're not putting any bad work out. There are a lot of checks and balances for each project. I also bring in second and third opinions and do a lot of fact-checking and research myself.

I do expect errors. And because I've worked so closely with AI for so long, I just pick up on them now. Something will feel off in the output and I'll catch it before it goes anywhere. It's like a sixth sense you develop from sitting in the work every single day.

Here's the thing that took me a while to understand: just because some things move faster doesn't mean you start taking shortcuts. It actually gives you more time to slow down and be deliberate. More time to think. More time to verify. More time to ask whether this is really the right approach.

I'm very familiar with what is and isn't possible at this point. What AI can do well, and where the tech just isn't there yet. That clarity only comes from repetition.

## the boring part that matters most

The part nobody talks about is the accountability and maintenance.

I work on many of my projects on a daily basis. Sometimes that's just merging pull requests. Sometimes it's diving into data. Sometimes it's adding a new feature. I check what's getting traffic. I look at error logs to make sure people are getting what they need without any problems.

This is the unsexy work. It's not building something new, it's keeping something alive. And it matters more than the launch ever did.

Here's what I've found: AI software is actually very stable once built successfully. I think that surprises people. If you can build it in an organized manner with scalability in mind, and you have great systems in place, good documentation, session logs, solid context awareness, it's far more stable than people realize. The projects I maintain daily aren't fragile. They just need attention, the way anything worth keeping does.

## hundreds of hours later

Eventually, after several projects under my belt, a multitude of projects in different domains, I feel like I could probably build about anything these days.

That's not arrogance. That's just what hundreds of hours of painful repetition earns you. From a user story generator to a Chrome extension to a synthesis engine to a journalism dashboard to a QR code tool to a mobile app. Every domain different, every stack different, every problem different. The thing that stayed the same was the process.

Write the spec first. Load the context. Verify the output. Document the session. Maintain what you ship. Repeat.

## do it right

I want to be clear about something. We don't need more junk.

The world doesn't need more half-built AI projects that work in a demo and fall apart in production. It doesn't need more people shipping things they don't understand because the tools made it easy to copy and paste.

If you're going to build with AI, put the effort in. Learn the best practices. Understand what you're shipping. Care about whether it actually works for the person on the other end. This work needs to be done thoughtfully, carefully.

I think people should feel empowered by what's possible right now. I genuinely do. But empowerment without discipline is just noise, and we have plenty of that already.
